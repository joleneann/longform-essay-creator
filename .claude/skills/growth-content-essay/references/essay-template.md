# Essay Template, Anti-Repetition, Depth, and Tone

There is no word cap. Write as much as the material demands. The section order is fixed.

## The 6-section structure

Use `#` for the title once, `##` for each section header (do NOT number section headers), and `###` for subsections inside Key Insights.

### 1. Source Info
Metadata directly under the title as `- **Label:** value` lines (this is the one place the meta pattern is correct): Title, Speaker/Author, Date if known, and a Source/Link line. For multiple sources, list each.

### 2. Executive Summary
4 to 6 sentences. State (a) what the source is and who the speaker is, (b) the central argument, and (c) why a growth practitioner should care. It is a map, not the territory. Do not put a statistic here that you want to use in Section 3 (see the stat-once rule).

### 3. Key Insights & Frameworks
The heart of the essay. Each major idea gets its own `###` subsection numbered 3.1, 3.2, and so on. These subsections are the topic inventory turned into prose. Every topic on the inventory must appear here with proportional depth. Capture the speaker's specific examples, anecdotes, numbers, company names, and caveats.

### 4. Tactical Playbook
Concrete moves a practitioner can make Monday morning. Each tactic cites the Section 3 subsection it derives from (e.g., "From 3.6:") but does NOT re-explain the underlying insight.

### 5. Contrarian / Non-Obvious Takes
3 to 5 items. Each is a one-sentence take plus 1 to 2 sentences of supporting reasoning that adds something NOT already covered in Section 3. If a contrarian point was fully developed in Section 3, leave it there.

### 6. Sources & Further Reading
A curated, annotated list of the best external sources found during research. Each entry: source name, URL, and one sentence on what it adds. Use markdown links `[title](url)`; md2pdf renders them as clean clickable text.

## Anti-repetition rules (mandatory)

1. **Single point of residence.** Every distinct idea, fact, statistic, quote, or anecdote has exactly ONE home section. Other sections cross-reference by subsection number.
2. **Quote once.** Each direct quote appears at most once in the whole essay.
3. **Stat once.** Each statistic appears at most once. If used in the Executive Summary as a hook, it must not reappear in Section 3.
4. **Cross-reference, do not restate.** When a later section builds on an earlier idea, write "(see 3.4)" then add NEW analysis only.
5. **Self-check before finalizing.** Scan for any idea, quote, stat, or anecdote that appears more than once and remove the duplicate.

## Traceability and provenance

Every claim in the essay should be traceable to where it came from, so the reader can verify it and so nothing is invented.

- **The topic inventory is the build-time audit trail.** The numbered inventory (with timestamps) maps each Section 3 subsection back to the exact place in the source. Keep it while writing so every subsection has a known origin.
- **Tag major claims with a source locator.** For video or audio sources, attach the timestamp to each significant claim, statistic, quote, or anecdote inline in Section 3 (e.g., "around [42:10]"), so the reader can jump to the moment it came from.
- **Attribute by source in multi-source essays.** When combining sources, make clear which source each claim came from (in Source Info, and inline wherever a point is specific to one source), so no claim is orphaned.
- **Cite every external claim.** Anything from research rather than the transcript must appear in Section 6 with a URL and be referenced where it is used.
- **No untraceable claims.** Do not introduce a fact, number, or quote that cannot be traced to the transcript or a cited source. This is the flip side of source fidelity: capture what the source said, attribute it, and add nothing that has no origin.

## Depth and completeness rules

The reader is choosing this essay INSTEAD OF watching the source. So:
- Capture the full substance and nuance. If the speaker spent 5 minutes on a topic, give it proportional depth. Do not flatten arguments into single sentences.
- Preserve qualifications and caveats. "Coding is largely solved, but with important exceptions" must not become "coding is solved."
- Include specific examples, anecdotes, data points, and named companies.
- Cover every major topic, not just the quotable ones. Cross-check against the topic inventory; any gap is a failure to fix before the PDF.
- "Introductory" material (analogies, use cases, motivating examples) is content, not filler. Include it.
- Multiple speakers or sources: include each perspective, woven by theme. Deduplicate overlap, keeping the richer version.
- Q&A sections contain real insights. Fold them into the relevant section.
- Never make editorial judgments about which sections "matter enough." If the speaker spent time on it, it goes in.

## Tone and style

- Analytical and practical, written for a practitioner.
- Plain, clear language, mechanism first, like explaining to a smart friend. Define jargon in a short clause on first use. Plain does not mean shallow.
- No fluff: every sentence earns its place.
- Original synthesis, not transcription: analyze, connect, contextualize.
- Use growth vocabulary naturally where it is the clearest term.
- NEVER use em dashes. Use colons, semicolons, commas, parentheses, or restructure.
